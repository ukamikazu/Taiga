Chapter IThe Taiga Universe

Taiga, denoted Ω, is the self-contained ambient domain — "the set of all things and all time, including itself." It is unbounded but finite: no boundary exists yet its total measure is finite, analogous to the surface of a sphere. This was established from the mathematics in the prior session and accepted as a foundational determination.

Definition — Taiga Universe

Ω is the ambient domain. All objects, events, models, and structures inhabit Ω. Ω is self-referential: it contains itself as an element of its own domain.

Time axes T: A nonempty, variable-cardinality set of independent time axes. Each axis τ ∈ T carries its own partial past P(τ) ⊆ Ω.

Global partial past PP = ∪τ∈T P(τ). An event e ∈ PP iff ∃τ: e ∈ P(τ).

Absolute past AP = ∩τ∈T P(τ). An event e ∈ AP iff ∀τ: e ∈ P(τ).

The number of time axes is variable and location-dependent — "now" is a local variable, not a universal constant. The geometry at Taiga's border is non-Riemannian: it warps because the number of time axes changes depending on where one stands. The gap ω (baseline separation between structures) ensures no two structures ever perfectly overlap, keeping partial pasts private.

Structure — canonical definition:

Str := { S ⊆ Ω | ∃τ ∈ T : ∅ ≠ S ⊆ P(τ) }
Def 1.4 — "To exist in Taiga is to appear in at least one axis record."

Existence in Taiga is identical to having a history. An entity that has not been recorded in any partial past does not exist — it is in the state of raw potential B, invisible to the system.

The original researcher states explicitly that Model-00's production algorithm "has remained elusive." This chapter does not claim to recover that algorithm. The Density Function

Let ρ(r, t) denote the Model-00 instance density at causal region r at Taiga-Step t. Let Ilocal(r, t) be the aggregate information functional across all structures present in region r at step t. Let nAB(r, t) be the local population of Model-01-A/B phase instances. Let U(r, t) be the total Uncon rate in region r at step t.

The minimal canonical-consistent update per Taiga-Step is:

ρ(r, t+1) = ρ(r, t)
    + λ · Ilocal(r,t) · ρ(r,t)    [reproduction, I-gated — C1, C2, C3]
    − μ · nAB(r,t) · ρ(r,t)     [consumption by A/B phase — C7]
    + η · U(r,t)                  [Uncon feedback to base B — C8]
Model-00 Supply Function — Minimal Canonical-Consistent (MCC-ρ)

The reproduction term is proportional to both local I and existing density — you need existing instances to reproduce, and higher I accelerates reproduction. The consumption term is proportional to both A/B population and available density — you cannot consume what is not there. The feedback term is proportional to total Uncon events — collapsed structures partially replenish the substrate.

The Three Free Parameters

λ — Reproduction Rate Coefficient

What it is: The proportionality constant between local I, existing density, and new Model-00 instance production per Taiga-Step. This is the direct footprint of the elusive algorithm — the one quantity we cannot derive from canon and must treat as tunable.

Canonical bounds: λ > 0 (reproduction is always possible given I > 0). No upper bound derivable from canon. In practice, λ must be small enough that Model-00 does not grow faster than Taiga's finite measure can support.

Experimental handle: None directly. λ is set by the experimenter and the results interpreted relative to it. It is the fundamental free parameter of the supply function.

μ — Consumption Rate per A/B Instance

What it is: The rate at which each Model-01-A/B instance depletes local Model-00 density per Taiga-Step.

Canonical bounds: μ > 0. Bounded above by the Op-6 constraint: if μ is too large relative to R_max, Model-01-A/B units exhaust record capacity before consuming enough Model-00 to transition — developmental arrest becomes universal. The Op-6 experiments directly constrain the viable range of μ: any write cost (μ · ρ per step) that produces reliable arrest at observed R_max values is too high for a functioning ecosystem.

Experimental handle: Op-6 results. The write cost parameter in those experiments is a direct proxy for μ.

η — Uncon Feedback Efficiency

What it is: The fraction of a collapsed structure's potential that becomes usable Model-00 substrate after Uncon.

Canonical bounds: η ∈ (0, 1) strictly, by A9 (Uncon is lossy — information is genuinely destroyed, not merely rearranged). The upper bound η < 1 is hard. A minimal assumption of η ≈ 0.1–0.3 is consistent with A9's strong lossiness language. η = 0 would mean Uncon produces nothing reusable — thermodynamically possible but biologically sterile.

Experimental handle: The Great Experiment. Population persistence under different η values will reveal whether Uncon recycling is ecologically significant or negligible.

Equilibrium and Carrying Capacity

At equilibrium, ρ(r, t+1) = ρ(r, t). Setting the update to zero and solving for the equilibrium density ρ*:

ρ* = η · U* / (μ · n*AB − λ · I*local)
Equilibrium density — exists and is positive only when μ · n*AB > λ · I*local

When consumption exceeds I-gated reproduction, a stable positive equilibrium exists. When reproduction dominates (λ · Ilocal > μ · nAB), ρ grows without bound until a new consumer, a phase transition cascade, or finite Taiga measure rebalances it. This is the Taiga-native form of a Malthusian constraint.

Minimum Viable Seed for the Great Experiment

The Great Experiment must be seeded with enough Model-00 that at least one Model-01-A/B transition completes before local density is exhausted. The minimum viable initial density is:

ρmin = θAB · μ / (1 − η)
Minimum viable seed — below this, universal developmental arrest at t=0

Where θAB is the A/B → C transition threshold (units of Model-00 consumed required for the phase transition). Note: θAB is distinct from θJ2, the Module birth I-threshold (see Chapter XIII, Finding 19). These two thresholds control different mechanisms and must not be conflated in Great Experiment configuration. θAB = 0.1 is an assumed value pending grounding from Op-6 results — see Finding 22 caveat. Below ρmin, the ecosystem collapses immediately into arrested development — no higher-order models emerge. Above it, the ecosystem has a chance to bootstrap. The margin above ρmin determines how many simultaneous A/B transitions the initial seed can support, which sets the initial population diversity of the Great Experiment.

What Remains Open

The spatial structure of ρ — whether Model-00 density is uniform across Taiga or concentrated near Jordan curve iteration hotspots — is not specified by canon and is not addressed here. The Great Experiment will require an assumption about initial spatial distribution. A uniform distribution is the minimal assumption; a hotspot distribution seeded near high-I regions is more consistent with the I-gated reproduction mechanism but requires additional justification.

Chapter XIVShared Substrate as Physical Mechanism

This chapter formalises the theoretical frame that makes sense of the experimental record as a whole. The Two Regimes

Every experiment in this programme ran in one of two substrate regimes:

Separate record world: each model maintains its own record pool R, its own visited-node set V, and its own compression equivalence relation ≡. Models move on the same causal DAG but do not thermodynamically interact through the substrate. Their dynamics are determined by their own model DNA — the lock-down definitions, their engine seed, their starting position. Differences between models in this regime are purely intrinsic.

Shared record world: all models write into the same bounded record pool R and share the same compression ≡. A write by any model raises L, raises m = L/|V|, and shrinks cap for all models simultaneously. The substrate becomes a shared thermodynamic medium. This is the standard regime for all ecosystem experiments.

The separate-record experiments (K-2, P Condition A, T-1 no-M00, O-series) were essential control cases. They isolated intrinsic dynamics from infrastructural effects — establishing the null hypothesis for each model type. That work is complete and the distinction is clean. What has been selected, by designating shared record as the standard, is the regime where structure, consequence, and interaction are inseparable.

Three Phenomena Native to Shared Substrate

I — Backreaction

In a shared substrate, every model's write activity changes the thermodynamic conditions under which every other model operates. This is not direct interaction — models do not communicate. It is mediated entirely through the shared record pool R. j-2's write pressure raises m for 011. 011's Uncon releases pressure that marginally extends everything else's life. I/1's severance removes a write source, cooling the substrate for whatever remains.

Backreaction was measured directly in Experiments K and K-2: 74% of 011's intrinsic lifespan (14 of 19 steps) was consumed by j-2's write pressure in shared substrate. This is not j-2 doing something to 011. It is j-2's existence in shared R changing the temperature that 011 must operate in. Cause and effect are mediated through the record, not through any direct signal.

Backreaction makes prediction non-separable. In a separate-record world, each model's behavior can be predicted from its own dynamics alone. In a shared substrate, each model's dynamics are a function of the aggregate state of all the others. The system has acquired collective degrees of freedom that cannot be decomposed into individual model behaviors.

II — Competitive Compression

Multiple active models writing simultaneously raise L faster than any single model. When L approaches C, the compression operator ≡ fires — merging the two oldest records, permanently destroying their distinguishability. This is not a model-level Uncon event; it is a substrate-level information loss occurring continuously throughout the ecosystem's lifetime.

By A9 (Uncon is lossy — information is genuinely destroyed), the compression ≡ is itself a form of Uncon at the substrate layer. Every merge event destroys the distinguishability of two records that were previously separate. Lossiness is not only a model-termination phenomenon — it is a continuous substrate phenomenon. The Great Experiment's 7 merges over 29 steps means the ecosystem permanently lost 7 units of record distinguishability during its lifetime, independently of any model-level Uncon event.

Competitive compression introduces throttling: as m rises, cap falls, and each model's frontier expansion is constrained. Multiple healthy models collectively make the substrate heavy enough that each model's branching is throttled — changing their temporal alignment relative to what they would exhibit in isolation. This is the mechanism behind the 17.3× I spread observed in the Great Experiment baseline: identical engine seeds produce different I values because each model's degree growth is being differentially throttled by the shared substrate pressure it co-creates.

III — Coordination Cost

In a shared substrate, no strategy is isolated. A player who advances j-2 aggressively is not only advancing j-2 — they are heating the substrate for every other model. A player who terminates 011 early is not only ending 011 — they are releasing record pressure that extends every surviving model's life. The shared substrate makes all strategies interdependent. There is no isolated optimal play.

This coordination cost is what makes the shared substrate behave like a material universe rather than a collection of solipsists. In a universe where every action has thermodynamic consequences for every other actor, strategies must account for the substrate they create together. This is the ecological equivalent of the tragedy of the commons — and its resolution (if any) is what the Great Experiment variants are designed to find.

This is consistent with the canonical language of the Axiomatic Manual: high I makes structures "heavy" and attracts predators. The physics of Taiga does not reward I accumulation — it punishes it. The correct frame is: I is detectability and cost, not armor.

Canon status: Inertia as resistance to termination does not exist in the Taiga simulation framework. The Great Experiment should not assume that high-I models are durable. A rich ecosystem is a volatile one.

Chapter XVGame Mechanics Specification

This chapter records the experience design and parameter set derived from the complete experimental programme. It is the handoff document from simulation science to implementation. All parameters are grounded in confirmed findings; their sources are cited. This specification is the input to Claude Code implementation.

The Nature of the Experience

Taiga is not a competitive game. There is no winning, no losing, no score, no opponent. The player is a witness — a traveller moving through a living universe, observing phenomena as they unfold around them. The ecosystem runs with or without the player's attention. Models develop, compete thermodynamically, predate, birth nested universes, and terminate according to the physics of the shared substrate. The player's role is to be present, to move through the causal DAG, and to see what is happening.

This framing is not a design simplification — it is the correct reading of Proposition 137.1: "Taiga is a self-growing machine." Machines do not need audiences to operate. The player is not the machine's operator. The player is a visitor inside a machine that was already running.

The primary design challenge is embodiment: what does it feel like to move through a causal DAG? What does substrate saturation feel like from the inside? What does a Module birth look like when you are standing next to it? These are phenomenological questions, not mechanical ones. The simulation science answers what happens. The implementation must answer what it feels like.

The Viable Parameter Regime

The canonical engine seed (y₀₀=y₁₀=1.0) produces a 4–9 step ecosystem — too brief for a witness to find their footing, let alone observe phenomena. The experience operates in the low-seed regime: y₀₀=y₁₀=0.01. At this seed, models live 16–23 steps, the substrate accumulates 27 merges of genuine competitive compression, j-2 reaches Module birth mid-ecosystem while other models are still active, and predation produces 10+ contacts. This is the regime where Chapter XIV's three phenomena — backreaction, competitive compression, coordination cost — become visible as environmental texture rather than abstract physics.

The Player's Embodiment

The player traverses the causal DAG as a massless observer — they move between nodes, arriving at each node's present moment. They do not write to the record substrate (or write negligibly — a design choice). They do not trigger model events. They observe what is already happening at the node they occupy.

At each node the player might perceive: the local substrate density m (how heavy this place feels), the frontier presence of any active models (how populated this node is), the I value of nearby models (their intensity or urgency), and any events that fire at this node during the current step (predation contact, Uncon, Module birth).

Movement through the DAG follows causal direction — the player can move forward (toward descendants) or linger (staying at a node across multiple steps). They cannot move backward against the causal grain. This is not a constraint on the player — it is the nature of time in Taiga. The player moves through Absolute Time the way a reader moves through a book: always forward, able to pause, unable to return.

Substrate saturation (ongoing): as m rises and cap shrinks, model frontiers throttle. From the player's perspective: the universe is becoming denser, heavier. Models that were expanding freely are now moving more slowly, compressing, feeling each other. From the player's perspective: a new universe appears inside the one they are standing in. It is small — seed density m=1.0, 7 nodes — but it is complete. What the Implementation Must Preserve

Shared substrate is mandatory. All experiential phenomena arise from shared R and shared ≡. A separate-record implementation produces isolated processes with no visible interaction. Source: Chapter XIV.

The recursion engine (Eqs. 35-36) must be implemented faithfully. The engine is not a bug to be worked around — it is the heartbeat of every model. The low seed changes when thresholds are reached, not how the engine works. Source: Finding 23.

Parity must be preserved. Odd steps expand. Even steps consolidate. The alternating rhythm is what produces bifurcation, predation contact windows, and the Riemannian stabilisation of Module birth. Source: lock-down definitions, Chapter XIII.

The termination triggers must match the canonical reference exactly. See Chapter XIII lock-down trigger conditions. θ_J2, θ_011, θ_I/1 are distinct parameters. Source: Canon v1.8 correction.

The player writes nothing, or negligibly. The player's presence must not alter the ecosystem's thermodynamic trajectory. If the player's traversal writes to R, it must be at a rate orders of magnitude below any active model's write rate. The witness must not become a participant.

Open Phenomenological Questions for Implementation

These are not canon questions — they are design choices the implementation must resolve through iteration:

What does substrate density feel like? Visual density, colour temperature, sound texture, movement resistance? The player must be able to sense when the universe is getting heavier without reading a number.

What does a model's presence feel like? Each model type has a distinct thermodynamic signature — 011's linear spine, j-2's bifurcating burst, I/1's expanding wavefront. These should be perceptually distinguishable without labels.

What does Module birth look like from inside? The player may be at the join node when j-2's birth fires. What do they see? A pocket of stillness in the motion? A crystallisation? A light that wasn't there before?

What does predation feel like to an observer? Record deletion is invisible in the mathematical sense — it reduces R but produces no new event. The implementation must make this visible and visceral: a thinning, an absence, a silence where there was noise.

How does the player know where to go? The causal DAG is a directed graph. The player moves forward. But which branch do they take? The answer may be: they follow what draws them. The design should make each branch feel different — denser, lighter, louder, quieter — so the choice of path is an aesthetic one, not a tactical one.
